Electricity Demand Analysis: Insights into Unmapped Factors for Reliable Forecast during Uncertainty

In just a few months of time, COVID-19 has brought a significant change in the way people do business. During this period, a lot of people have experienced a change in their working habits and there has been a significant change observed in the economic activity in Australia. We attempt to detect this shift using electricity demand data as it is the most updated dataset currently available. The policymakers and electricity operators must adopt a cross-domain approach to assess and predict electricity demand. Here we attempt to integrate traditional weather data with data related to COVID-19 to discover patterns in the change in the demand for electricity during COVID-19.

Actual v/s Predicted Electricity Demand Using Pre- COVID Model

Let’s start understanding how the electricity demand has changed during the COVID period (March - Aug 2020) in Australia. For our analysis, we have selected the top 2 COVID hotspots; Victoria and NSW. Below is the graph which compares Actual Electricity Demand with Predicted Electricity Demand using LSTM regression model. Our prediction model trained from 1/1/2016 to 30/12/2019 using weather and climate data as a predictor variable shows that there is a modest change in the demand for electricity in Victoria with slightly higher change observed in NSW. The average percentage change (predicted – actual) from March – Aug 2020 for Victoria shows a decline of 2.1% while this percentage is 2.4% for NSW.
High demand change can be attributed to stricter restrictions in NSW. Although this percentage was expected to be significant due to shut down of the commercial and industrial activity, much of the change seems to be offset by high residential consumption as more people have started working from home.

Insight into Factors influencing the demand during COVID-19

To interpret the changes in electricity demand, we start by investigating two major factors, namely social distancing and public health. In Victoria, we used Melbourne Pedestrian count data to represent social distancing while in New South Wales, the same factor is represented by Google trend data of keywords related to daily outdoor activities performed by people. The chosen keywords are; ‘Train’, ‘Event’, and ‘hotel’. Social Distancing is measured by calculating the inverse of cumulative counts. Figure 3: shows variations in time scales of different factors influencing electricity consumption during COVID -19. Electricity demand started dropping almost immediately after the government put restrictions in place. Following this, the number of new confirmed cases started increasing rapidly with a major spike observed on 3 March 2020. It is also observed that electricity demand started rising almost immediately after a decline in the number of confirmed cases and ease of restrictions.

In NSW, fig 3, electricity demand shows an early decline with respect to increase in the number of confirmed cases of COVID-19. Further, electricity demand showcased a delayed rebound in comparison to social distance measures with respect to increase in number of COVID-19 confirmed cases. Considering these features, we are aimed at quantifying this multi-dimensional relationship by modelling change (predicted - actual) in electricity demand using a Vector Autoregressive Model (VAR). Analysis of variance decomposition in VAR model indicates the percentage contribution of the above mentioned factors to change in electricity demand. In NSW, social distancing seems to account for significant proportion of the change in electricity demand during COVID months.

Impulse response function assessing the sensitivity of COVID related factors to the change in electricity demand

The fluctuations in the impulse response is the representative of sensitivity to electricity change with respect to change in factors. Victoria represents that the change in electricity demand is most sensitive to change in the Melbourne pedestrian count with fluctuations in count lasting longer. Further, the number of confirmed cases does not seem to be a much stronger influencing factor to the change in electricity demand.

In NSW social distancing seems to contribute much higher percentage over weather-related factors. Further, despite showing a larger contribution to the change in electricity demand during FEVD analysis, it is not necessary that the factor will provide high sensitivity in impulse response analysis. For instance, in NSW, the number of confirmed cases seems to contribute a significant share to the change in electricity demand but shows very little sensitivity to the change in electricity demand in impulse response analysis. This gives another useful insight that any measure alone cannot be used to assess the impact on change in electricity demand but both measures taken together will help to assess the influence.

Conclusion

Our findings are well able to measure the dynamics between the number of COVID confirmed cases, people’s changing behaviour and government policies across different states in different time scales Thus we conclude that traditional forecasting methods must be used together with such multi-dimensional analysis to assess the impact on the demand of electricity during uncertain times such as COVID-19.
